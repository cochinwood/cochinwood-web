Why the core matters more than the face

When buyers compare plywood, they usually look at the face veneer — okoume, gurjan, a clean paintable surface. The face is mostly cosmetic. The core is the stack of inner veneers that makes up most of the panel's thickness, and it is the core that decides how the sheet actually performs. Weight, screw and nail holding, stiffness and strength all come from the core species, not the face. Two sheets with the same okoume face can behave completely differently depending on what is underneath. So when you specify plywood, specify the core.

Hardwood core (mixed tropical hardwood / gurjan)

A hardwood core is the densest of the common options. It gives the best screw-holding and the highest strength, and it is the heaviest. This is the core you want where the panel has to carry load, take repeated fastening or survive rough handling — packing cases and crates for heavy goods, marine and structural panels, and heavy-duty work generally. The tradeoff is weight: a hardwood-core panel adds the most dead weight to a case or a piece of furniture.

Eucalyptus core

Eucalyptus is a plantation-grown hardwood. It offers good density and strength with consistent, renewable supply, which is why it has become a common modern core. It sits between hardwood and poplar — it can approach hardwood on strength and screw-hold depending on species and grade, but is more uniform and more sustainably sourced. Our calibrated Okoume furniture grade is built on a eucalyptus core for exactly this reason: it gives a stable, predictable panel for furniture and interiors without the inconsistency of mixed wild hardwood.

Poplar core

Poplar is botanically a hardwood, but a light, low-density one. It holds screws less well and is less strong than hardwood or eucalyptus, but it is flat, stable and noticeably lighter. Choose poplar where weight is the priority and the panel does not carry much load — light furniture, craft and display work, and some interior applications. It is the wrong core for heavy crating or anything that must hold a fastener under stress.

How to choose a core for your job

  • Crating heavy or engineering goods? Specify a hardwood core for maximum strength and screw-hold.
  • Building furniture or interior panels? A eucalyptus core gives a consistent, renewable, well-balanced panel.
  • Weight is the main constraint and load is light? A poplar core keeps the panel light and flat.
  • Marine use? Stay with a hardwood core, and confirm the grade and bonding to the IS 710 / BWP marine specification. For structural work, match the grade and bonding to your application's own specification rather than the marine standard.

The core sits behind the grade and the certificate, so always confirm the core species to your spec when you order — the face alone does not tell you how the panel will perform. FAQ

Does the plywood core or the face veneer matter more?

For performance, the core. The core makes up most of the panel's thickness and drives weight, screw-holding, stiffness and strength. The face veneer is mostly cosmetic — it sets the look and how the surface paints or finishes, not how the panel carries load.

Which plywood core is strongest?

A hardwood core (mixed tropical hardwood or gurjan) is the densest and strongest, with the best screw and nail holding. It is also the heaviest, which is the main tradeoff.

Why is eucalyptus used as a plywood core?

Eucalyptus is a plantation hardwood that gives good density and strength with consistent, renewable supply. That balance of performance and uniformity makes it a common modern core, including for our calibrated Okoume furniture grade.

When should I choose a poplar core?

Choose poplar when light weight matters more than load. It is light and flat but holds screws less well and is less strong, so it suits light furniture, craft and display work and some interiors — not heavy crating or high-stress fastening.

Which core is best for packing cases and crates?

A hardwood core, in most cases. Packing cases for heavy or engineering goods need the strength and screw-hold that only a dense hardwood core provides. Where the case is light-duty and weight is the priority, a lighter core can be considered.
