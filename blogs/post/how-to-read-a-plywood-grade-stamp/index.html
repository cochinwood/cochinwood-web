How to Read a Plywood Grade Stamp — and Spot a Fakeable Claim

Cochin Wood Industries

Short answer: a genuine plywood marking names the IS standard it is built to (IS 303 for commercial, IS 710 for marine, IS 4990 for shuttering), states the bond grade (MR, BWR or BWP), and — if it is ISI-certified — carries the ISI mark with a licence number, typically in the CM/L-xxxxxxx format that can be cross-checked. What is fakeable: a grade ink-stamped on the face with nothing behind it, an "ISI-style" symbol with no licence number, or grade letters with no test certificate. Reading a plywood grade stamp: claim versus proof

A plywood face carries an ink-stamped grade, which is only a claim. Real proof is a third-party test certificate plus an ISI mark with a checkable licence number. The grade lives in the glue line and core, not the face veneer.

What a plywood marking is actually telling you

Every sheet of branded plywood carries printing on the face and along the edge. A buyer who can read that printing can separate a real, testable grade claim from a stamp that means nothing. A genuine marking points to three things you can check: the Indian Standard (IS) the panel is made to, the bond grade of the glue line, and — when the product is ISI-certified — an ISI mark with a licence number.

The order of trust matters. A standard number and a grade letter printed on the face are claims. A licence number and a third-party test certificate are evidence. We deal in this distinction every day on the buying side, so the guidance below is what our export desk uses before a panel goes into a packing case or a container.

The IS standard reference

The first thing to look for is the IS number, because it tells you which test regime the panel is claiming to meet. The three you will see most often:

  • IS 303 — commercial plywood (the general-purpose grade, MR or BWR bonded).
  • IS 710 — marine plywood, the boiling-water-proof grade.
  • IS 4990 — concrete shuttering / formwork plywood.

A marking that quotes an IS number is making a specific, checkable claim — that panel should have a test certificate behind it tying it to that standard. A marking with a grade word but no IS number at all is weaker, and should make you ask more questions.

The bond grade — and why it lives in the glue, not the face

The bond grade describes how the glue line behaves when it gets wet. This is the single most important property for packing, crating and any humid or outdoor use, and it is decided by the resin in the glue line and the core — not by the species on the surface.

Bond grade Stands for Roughly what it means Typical standard
MR Moisture Resistant Resists humidity and occasional damp; not for soaking or boiling IS 303
BWR Boiling Water Resistant Withstands prolonged damp and limited boiling exposure IS 303
BWP Boiling Water Proof Glue line survives prolonged boiling; the marine class IS 710

This is where most buyers get caught. A panel can carry a beautiful Okoume or Gurjan face and still be a low-bond board if the glue is urea-based. The face veneer is cosmetic; the grade is in the glue line and the core. So treat a premium face species as a separate line item from the bond grade, and never let one stand in for the other.

The ISI mark and licence number

If a panel is genuinely ISI-certified, it carries the ISI monogram together with a licence number. That licence number is typically in the form CM/L-xxxxxxx (confirm the exact format on the sheet you are holding, as presentation varies). The point of the licence number is that it is not just decoration — it identifies the certified manufacturer and licence, and can be cross-checked rather than taken on faith.

An "ISI-style" symbol with no licence number is one of the clearest warning signs you can find. The monogram on its own is easy to print; the licence number is the part that ties the mark to an actual certification. No number, no real claim.

How to verify a grade claim before you buy

Three checks, in order of weight:

  • Ask for the third-party lab test certificate. This is the strongest evidence. It ties the panel to glue-shear and boil-test numbers under the relevant standard. For the marine class, our guide on how to read an IS 710 BWP test certificate walks through exactly which numbers to look for and which lines get faked.
  • Check the ISI licence number. If the mark carries a licence number, confirm it is present, legible and in a plausible CM/L format — then cross-check it rather than assuming the monogram is enough.
  • Read the glue line, not the face. Remember the grade is decided by the bond and the core. A good-looking face proves nothing about the boil performance.

FAQ

What do MR, BWR and BWP mean on plywood?

They are bond grades describing how the glue line handles moisture. MR is Moisture Resistant (resists humidity, not soaking), BWR is Boiling Water Resistant (withstands prolonged damp and limited boiling), and BWP is Boiling Water Proof — the marine class where the glue line survives prolonged boiling. The grade lives in the glue and core, not the face veneer.

Which IS standard should a real grade marking reference?

Commercial plywood references IS 303, marine plywood references IS 710, and concrete shuttering plywood references IS 4990. A marking that quotes one of these is making a checkable claim, which should be backed by a matching test certificate. A grade word with no IS number behind it is a weaker claim worth questioning.

How can I tell a genuine ISI mark from a fake one?

A genuine ISI mark is accompanied by a licence number, typically in a CM/L-xxxxxxx format (confirm the exact format on the sheet, as it can vary). That number identifies the certified licence and can be cross-checked. An ISI-style symbol with no licence number is a clear warning sign — the monogram alone is easy to print, so the number is the part that matters.

Is a grade stamped on the face proof of quality?

No. An ink-stamped grade on the face is a claim, not evidence. Anyone can print MR, BWR or BWP. Proof comes from the third-party lab test certificate that ties the panel to glue-shear and boil-test results under the relevant IS standard, plus a checkable ISI licence number if the product is ISI-certified.

Does a premium Okoume or Gurjan face mean the plywood is a high grade?

No. The face species is cosmetic and is sold as a face option. The bond grade is decided by the resin in the glue line and the condition of the core. A panel can have a premium face and still be a low-bond board if the glue is urea-based, so always read the grade and certificate separately from the face.
